An intrinsic semiconductor at room temperature has sufficient heat energy for some valence electrons to jump the gap from the valence band into the conduction band, becoming free electrons.

When a voltage is applied across a piece of silicon, the thermally generated free electrons in the conduction band, which are free to move randomly in the crystal structure, are now easily attracted towards the positive end. This movement of free electrons in a semiconductive material is called electron current.

Electrons remaining in the valence band are still attached to their atoms and are not free to move randomly in the crystal structure. However, a valence electron can move into a nearby hole, with little change in its energy level, thus leaving another hole where it came from. The hole has effectively, although not physically, moved from one place to another in the crystal structure. This current is called hole current.

The valence band and the conduction band in a conductor overlap so that there are always many conduction electrons, even without application of external energy where as the energy gap for an insulator is so wide that hardly any electrons acquire enough energy to jump into the conduction band.

Thus the energy is more easily established in a semiconductor than in an insulator.
