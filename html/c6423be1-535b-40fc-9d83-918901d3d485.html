QUESTION 2.

Again assuming that V is a vector space over |R.

A. TRUE.
     Any two bases of a vector space have the same number of elements.

B.  FALSE.
      If V is not {0} and V is spanned by a finite set S, then V is indeed finite dimensional. However, S may be linearly
      dependent. As a result, S may not be a basis of V, in which case, a proper subset of S is a basis of V.
      For instance, |R2 is spanned by the set S={ (1,0),(0,1),(1,2) } . However, S is not a basis of |R2, being
      |R-linearly dependent.

C.  TRUE.
      This is the definition of the dimension of a finite-dimensional vector space V.

D.  FALSE.
      Span{u,v} may be a one-dimensional subspace of |R3 for some vectors u,v in |R3.
      For instance, let u=(1,1,1) and v=(3,3,3) in |R3.
      Then, v=3u. Thus, Span{u,v}={ au+bv : a,b are in |R } = {au+3bu : a,b are in |R} = {(a+3b)u : a,b are in |R}
                                                    ={cu : c is in |R} =Span{u}. Thus in this situation, Span{u,v} is one-dimensional.

E.  TRUE.
      If V is finite dimensional and L is a linearly independent set, then L can always be extended to a basis of V using the
      following algorithm:
:
      STEP 1: Consider Span(L). If Span(L)=V, go to STEP 4. Otherwise, go to STEP 2.
      STEP 2: If V \ Span(L) is non-empty, there exists a v in V\Span(L). Consider L1=L   {v}.
      STEP 3: Put L=L1. Go to STEP 1.
      STEP 4: END.
 
      Since, V is finite dimensional, hence the above algorithm terminates at a finite stage and the resulting extension
      of L is a basis of V.

F.  TRUE.
      Suppose that L is a linearly independent set of p vectors. If Span(L) is a proper subset of V, then there is a
      v in V \ Span(L). Then L    {v} is a linearly independent set of p+1 vectors. This is a contradiction as the dimension of V
      is p and therefore, V can have linearly independent sets of cardinality at most p. Hence, Span(L)=V and thus, L is a basis.

      Similarly, let S be a spanning set of p vectors. Suppose that S is not linearly independent. Then some proper subset of 
      S is linearly independent and spans V. Thus, some proper subset of S(having less than p elements) is a basis of V.
      This is a contradiction as the dimension of V is p for which any basis of V has exactly p elements.
      Thus, S is |R-linearly independent and hence, a basis.
